    <h2 class="research-paper-title"><a href="https://doi.org/10.5194/egusphere-egu26-17766" target="_blank" style="color: inherit;">Hydrodynamic Changes of Estuarine Islands in the Meghna River</a></h2>
    <p style="margin: -0.3rem 0 0.7rem;"><img src="/assets/img/EGU26_logo.jpg" alt="EGU26" style="height: 1.6em; vertical-align: middle; margin-right: 0.5em; border-radius: 3px;"><span style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);">EGU General Assembly 2026</span></p>
    <p>This study analyses long-term hydrodynamic changes across seven estuarine islands — <strong>Nijhum Dwip, Moulovi Char, Domar Char, Char Kukri Mukri, Rajrajeshwar, Hatiya</strong>, and <strong>Manipura</strong> — in the Meghna Estuary under future climate scenarios.</p>
    <p>Future discharge for 2050 was generated from <strong>MIROC6</strong> climate projections under <strong>SSP2-4.5</strong> and <strong>SSP5-8.5</strong> scenarios, bias-corrected and routed through <strong>HEC-HMS</strong>. A <strong>Delft3D</strong> hydrodynamic model, spanning Baruriya Transit to the sea, was calibrated against observed water level and discharge records and run for 2000, 2024, and 2050.</p>
    <p>Results show high tides near Char Kukri Mukri increasing by <strong>30–35 cm</strong> by 2050, with average water depth rises of <strong>0.5–0.8 m</strong> around Rajrajeshwar, Hatiya, and Manipura — accelerating shoreline erosion and land loss along their southern and eastern margins.</p>
    <p style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);"><em>Presented — <a href="https://doi.org/10.5194/egusphere-egu26-17766" target="_blank">DOI: 10.5194/egusphere-egu26-17766</a></em></p>
  </div>

    <h2 class="research-paper-title"><a href="https://doi.org/10.5194/egusphere-egu26-13958" target="_blank" style="color: inherit;">Post-Flood Sediment Deposition Analysis Using SAR Interferometry</a></h2>
    <p style="margin: -0.3rem 0 0.7rem;"><img src="/assets/img/EGU26_logo.jpg" alt="EGU26" style="height: 1.6em; vertical-align: middle; margin-right: 0.5em; border-radius: 3px;"><span style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);">EGU General Assembly 2026</span></p>
    <p>This study quantifies post-flood sediment deposition in the <strong>Feni floodplain</strong>, Bangladesh, following the catastrophic August 2024 flood triggered by overflow from the <strong>Dumboor dam</strong> in Tripura state.</p>
    <p><strong>SAR Interferometry</strong> using <strong>Sentinel-1</strong> SLC products was applied in <strong>SNAP</strong> to generate Digital Elevation Models before and after the flood. Eight cross-section profiles in <strong>HEC-RAS</strong> revealed an average sediment deposition of <strong>2.7 cm</strong> and a maximum of <strong>7.5 cm</strong> across the region.</p>
    <p style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);"><em>Presented — <a href="https://doi.org/10.5194/egusphere-egu26-13958" target="_blank">DOI: 10.5194/egusphere-egu26-13958</a></em></p>

    <h2 class="research-paper-title"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/s41024-025-00632-6" target="_blank" style="color: inherit;">Structural Dynamics of Cooling Towers</a></h2>
    <p style="margin: -0.3rem 0 0.7rem;"><img src="/assets/img/springer_logo.png" alt="Springer" style="height: 1.6em; vertical-align: middle; margin-right: 0.5em;"><span style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);">Journal of Building Pathology and Rehabilitation, Springer</span></p>
    <p>My primary research investigates the dynamic behaviour of large-scale hyperbolic cooling towers under seismic and wind loading, with a focus on the <strong>Rooppur Nuclear Power Plant</strong>, Bangladesh's first nuclear power plant, located near the seismically active Madhupur Fault.</p>
    <p>Using <strong>ABAQUS</strong>, I developed and validated finite element models of the 175 m tower supported by 100 Λ-shaped reinforced concrete columns. Two column modelling strategies were compared: an <strong>Embedded Reinforcement</strong> approach and a <strong>Simplified Timoshenko Beam</strong> approach. Both models were subjected to the 2001 Bhuj/Kachchh earthquake via non-linear time history analysis and to wind loads calculated using Greiner's equation per BNBC-2020.</p>
    <p>The Embedded Reinforcement model exhibited greater flexibility, forming plastic hinges and cracks under seismic loading, resulting in larger displacements but lower base shear and stresses. Maximum radial displacement was 38 mm (seismic) and 15 mm (wind).</p>
    <p style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);"><em>Published — Journal of Building Pathology and Rehabilitation, Springer, 2025</em></p>
  </div>

    <h2 class="research-paper-title">Impact Behaviour of Pressurised uPVC Pipes</h2>
    <p>This study investigates the low-velocity lateral impact response of water-pressurised <strong>uPVC pipes</strong> under two support conditions such as, <strong>both-ends-clamped (CC)</strong> and <strong>one-clamped-one-roller (CR)</strong>, tested at four drop heights (1.0, 1.5, 2.0, and 2.75 m) using a <strong>locally developed ESP32-based pressure and velocity measuring unit</strong> capable of recording ~18–20 pressure samples per millisecond.</p>
    <p>Eight Class-D uPVC pipe specimens (1" diameter, 300 mm clear span) were pressurised to 1378.9 kPa and struck at midspan by a 4.0 kg cylindrical hammer. Peak internal pressure reached <strong>3063 kPa</strong>. Clamped supports produced up to <strong>6.34% higher contact force</strong>. FEA matched experiment within <strong>14%</strong>.</p>
    <p style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);"><em>Under Review, 2025</em></p>

    <h2 class="research-paper-title">CFRP-Strengthened Beam-Column Steel Joints</h2>
    <p>This study investigates the behaviour of mild steel beam-column T-joints retrofitted with <strong>Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer (CFRP)</strong> under both monotonic and cyclic loading, using ABAQUS finite element analysis validated against experimental data.</p>
    <p>Three section profiles, <strong>Square Hollow, I-Section</strong>, and <strong>Circular Hollow</strong>, were modelled with and without CFRP wrapping. Under monotonic loading, CFRP retrofitting improved load-carrying capacity by up to <strong>26.71%</strong>. Under cyclic loading, hysteresis analysis showed energy dissipation gains of up to <strong>33.20%</strong>.</p>
    <p style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: var(--global-text-color-light);"><em>Under Review — Advances in Civil Engineering, 2025</em></p>
